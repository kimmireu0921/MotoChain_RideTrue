II.2 — Zero-Knowledge Proof Architecture

How RideTrue scores behavior without exposing raw data

1. Collect

Mobile app records GPS, accelerometer, gyroscope, and ECU data during rides.

2. Compute

Off-chain processor evaluates behavioral metrics: speed adherence, braking patterns, cornering.

3. Prove

ZK proof generated — attests "rider met safety conditions" without exposing raw location data.

4. Submit

Proof submitted on-chain. Smart contract verifies it. No raw data ever stored on-chain.

5. Update

Safety score updated. Premium recalculated. Rebate eligibility determined automatically.

II.3 — Interface Design

Three distinct interfaces, one unified protocol

Rider dashboard

Safety score, current premium level, and projected savings shown on a single screen. Behavioral tracking runs passively in the background — no active input needed during rides. Rebate eligibility, score history, and pool contribution visible at a glance.

Mechanic interface

QR-code scan identifies the vehicle instantly. Structured form captures service type, mileage, parts, and cost in a fixed sequence designed for speed. Owner co-signs via the rider app through a QR handoff at the shop. End-to-end flow: under 30 seconds per entry.

Buyer interface

Scan a code or enter a VIN to access a complete, cryptographically verified history — ownership transfers, service records, mechanic reputation, and claim events — with no account, wallet, or crypto knowledge required.

Design constraint: every interface must be usable by someone who has never heard of blockchain. Account abstraction and gasless transactions eliminate all crypto-native friction from all three flows.

II.5 — Mechanic Trust: Prevention Layer

Eliminate fraud categories before they can happen

Multi-party co-signing

Every service record requires cryptographic signatures from both the mechanic and the bike owner. The owner signs via the rider app through a QR-code handoff at the time of service. This eliminates all unilateral mechanic fraud — fabricating service for a bike never at the shop, falsifying mileage without owner consent, or inflating cost without agreement — and creates a signed evidentiary trail the owner cannot later disclaim.

Hardware-attested telemetry

OBD-II adapters, paired Bluetooth modules, and OEM telematics where available automatically read and cryptographically sign mileage and repair-trigger events at the source. Hardware-attested entries are flagged in the history record and weighted more heavily by risk scoring. High-value downstream use cases — resale appraisal, loan underwriting, insurance tier eligibility — can require hardware attestation, creating commercial pressure for adoption.

II.5 — Mechanic Trust: Detection Layer

Surface fraud that prevention cannot stop

Bounty market

Any third party — competing mechanics, dealers, downstream buyers, professional auditors, or algorithmic bots — can submit evidence of a fraudulent entry and earn a share of slashed stake and clawed-back fees if the evidence is verified. Simple algorithmic catches earn modest bounties; substantive evidence earns larger shares. This converts fraud detection from a cost center into a revenue opportunity, attracting the only category of actor with both capability and incentive to surface collusion: independent third parties.

Trigger-on-monetization verification

A re-verification step fires automatically at every downstream monetization event — resale, trade-in, insurance underwriting, loan origination. The protocol cross-checks against police records, insurance claim databases, OEM service backends, and OBD historical reads. Discrepancies trigger retroactive dispute proceedings even after the original escrow has released, because the mechanic's accumulated stake and reputation remain at risk indefinitely. This extends the effective detection window from the 30–90 day escrow period to the entire commercial life of the bike.

II.5 — FX Risk Management

KRW pricing vs. USDC on-chain

The structural exposure

Premiums are priced in Korean won (₩) for the rider-facing market, but pool contributions and on-chain balances are held in USDC. A KRW depreciation reduces the local purchasing power of reserves; appreciation increases liability coverage but compresses realized yield in won terms.

Protocol mitigation

A portion of reserves is held in KRW-pegged stablecoins where reliably available. The protocol tracks both a USD-equivalent and a KRW-denominated reserve ratio. Yield deployment is restricted when FX volatility exceeds governance thresholds. Formal hedging is deferred post-launch once pool scale justifies the operational cost.

The DeFi yield cap is a ceiling, not a target — only excess reserves above the 25% liquidity floor and 20% catastrophe floor are eligible for deployment. Insurance capital is not speculative capital.

II.5 — Non-Utility Rule

$MOTO is coordination infrastructure — not a yield instrument

What $MOTO does

Staking and slashing for write privileges. Governance voting on protocol parameters — fees, reserve ratios, rebate formulas, treasury deployment, supported integrations. Reputation gating for tiered mechanic write limits. Fee discounts on selected registration and API fees when paid in $MOTO, with protocol burning or treasury accrual of received tokens.

What $MOTO does NOT do

Insurance pool returns, rider rebates, and all protocol revenue distributions are paid in USDC — not in $MOTO. This is intentional. Projects that pay yield in their own token create reflexive demand; when sentiment reverses, the loop collapses. Paying real yield in stablecoins from real protocol revenue breaks that dependency and reduces the risk of securities classification.

II.6 — Security Engineering

Pre-launch gate and independent audit standards

Pre-launch engineering gate

≥90% unit and integration test coverage for all fund-handling contracts. Property-based and invariant testing for accounting, staking, slashing, and payout flows. Fuzz testing via Echidna or Foundry invariant fuzzing. Static analysis via Slither with zero unresolved high-severity findings before audit handoff. Internal threat modeling reviewed by the core team and at least one external security advisor.

External audits

Minimum two independent smart-contract audits before mainnet deployment. Target firms: OpenZeppelin, Trail of Bits, ChainSecurity, Quantstamp, Spearbit, Code4rena competitive audit. At least one audit must include formal verification of accounting, escrow, and slashing logic. All reports published in full — including unresolved findings and team responses.

Bug bounty (Immunefi)

Hosted on Immunefi from mainnet launch and live for the lifetime of the protocol. Indicative reward bands: Critical up to $250,000 · High up to $75,000 · Medium up to $15,000 · Low up to $2,500. Annual budget funded from the treasury allocation.

Operational security

Treasury and admin functions controlled by a 5-of-9 multi-sig with geographically distributed signers. Time-locked upgrades: 48-hour minimum for any non-emergency parameter change. Emergency pause restricted to narrow scope with documented invocation criteria. Public incident response runbook with quarterly drills. Annual third-party penetration testing for mobile client and telematics ingestion systems.
